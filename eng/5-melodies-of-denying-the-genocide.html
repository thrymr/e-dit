5 Melodies of Denying the Genocide

Ali Topuz – Radikal, 24.04.2012

It is difficult to talk about 1915, but impossible to shut up about it. There are two ways that make it easy to talk about 1915; the state-preferred way of denial, and mourning that is preferred by those who do not want to be partners of the genocide crime.

Those who prefer denial use the “we should leave it to the historians” argument every time they are cornered with discussions. “Those who say this know well enough that history is more important than merely leaving it to the historians. That is why they immediately add ‘our ancestors would commit genocide’.”

All known attempts of genocide are organized by the state or a similar organization and are carried out by that. “One example is enough: The Nazi atrocities were not blamed upon all Germans.” That is why saying that there was a genocide in 1915 puts forth the attitudes and behaviors of the statesmen at the time. Defending ourselves with a rhetoric that our ancestors would not do something like that is prohibiting any discussion even before it starts. This argument carries all the codes of the ideological Turk-Islam synthesis doctrine that started being taught during the time of Abdulhamit in 1915 and was revised and updated after September 12, 1980 (coup).

Also, saying that our ancestors would not commit genocide is saying that the Armenians that lived here once upon a time are not our ancestors. While this immediately excludes the Armenian citizens in the country, it also discloses the codes of the 1915 mentality; “There must be two ethnicities in order for genocide to happen: a strong one affiliated with the government to be praised and protected, and a weak one to be destroyed and despised. A race that is not from us, and not us.”

Another group argues that genocide is a legal term, and that the legal terms could not be employed backwards. Since there was no such terminology prior to the Holocaust, the events of 1915 could not be defined as genocide either, according to them.

This kind of “extremely positivist” thinking would do nothing but commence an Olympiad of thoughts that would make it impossible to deal with crimes against humanity, and, naturally, against genocide.

Then there are those who say that the imperialist powers are exploiting the issue. There is nothing that the imperialist powers do not exploit. “Exactly for this reason it should be mandatory to oppose the denial of 1915: Because opposing 1915 is opposing the homogenizing nation-state mentality, and, therefore, opposing imperialism and its spirit.

Then there is the blaming of the Armenian Diaspora. When this is mentioned, it is as though someone is talking about the devil. One of the most common forms of mentioning the Diaspora is: “Actually, it is not difficult for us to get along with Armenia, but this Diaspora…” And another one: “We do not have any problems with our Armenians, but this Diaspora…”

Talking about Diaspora and ‘our Armenians’ in different terms is like saying Armenians are not from us, or that they belong to us.

There is only one condition that would make it possible to criticize a group’s demand for their historic right for having been subjected to destruction; “Stop denying 1915, open the path to return, announce that you are ready for re-compensation, ask for forgiveness, and mourn. Then nobody would need the ‘devil Diaspora’ fairytale anyway.
