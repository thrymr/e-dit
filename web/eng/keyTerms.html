Political Parties in the National Assembly

CHP (The Republican People’s Party): Founded by Ataturk and his comrades, CHP dominated Turkey’s political sphere until 1946 when Turkey started the transition to a “multiple-party” democratic system. The party has maintained a secular/Kemalist line to this day, and is therefore in strong opposition to the arrest of former army generals. Also, CHP is currently the main opposition party in the National Assembly. Branded as a “social democrat” party, CHP has had troubles over the past decades to have a strong say in the government. However, the party’s solid voter base allows them to enter the National Assembly in every election. Chairman: Kemal Kilicdaroglu. Political Affiliation: Social Democrat, Center Left.

AKP (Justice and Development Party): Founded in 2001 by the incumbent PM Recep Tayyip Erdogan, AKP has been in power since the November 2002 elections. The party’s founding members were those who were kicked out of office after the “post-modern” coup of February 28, 1997 – when the rule of the Welfare Party came to an end. The Islamist party divided into two wings after the coup – the traditionalist wing (hard line conservative Islamists) and the reformists (liberal Islamists). The founders of AKP comprised the reformist wing of the Welfare Party, and upon their resignation from the party, they founded their own. Although their liberal democratic approach to politics commenced a new era in Turkey, the hopes of ending the military domination in politics have now transformed into a fear of AKP domination and oppression. Chairman: Recep Tayyip Erdogan. Political Affiliation: Liberal Islamist, Center Right.

BDP (Peace and Democracy Party): Strongly opposed to assimilation, BDP is known for its outspoken criticism of the government’s rejection to grant the Kurds equal rights as the Turks. Among these rights are those such as education in mother tongue, accepting Kurdish along with Turkish as the official language of the country, granting democratic autonomy to the southeastern region of Turkey and more. The parliamentarians from BDP include former columnists like Sirri Sureyya Onder, renowned socialist activists of the 1970s like Ertugrul Kurkcu and other Kurdish businessmen. While no organic connection between the BDP and the PKK has been proven, the party refuses to articulate the PKK as a terrorist organization, thus receiving criticism from the Turkish circles for “favoring violence against the state.” Many Kurdish affiliated political parties have been shut down by the Turkish courts, last of which was the DTP (Democratic Society Party) in 2009. Chairman: Selahattin Demirtas. Political Affiliation: Liberal Democrat, Left.

MHP (The Nationalist Movement Party): Founded in 1969, the party follows a strict Turkish nationalist and Islamist policy line. Although their presence in the Turkish political sphere has been less than impressive over the past decade, the party saw a rise in the number of votes they r eceived in the last elections. However, due to the new election law, this increase in the number of votes was not reflected in the number of parliamentarians they manage to put in the Assembly, on the contrary, bringing it down. While MHP groups took occasional violent approaches to the Kurdish issue, the party has taken a quiet stance toward ethnic issues in Turkey since their current chairman became the party leader in 1997. Chairman: Devlet Bahceli. Political Affiliation: Conservative, Far Right.


The Controversial Topics of Debate in Turkey

The Ergenekon Trial: “Ergenekon” is the name given to an alleged Kemalist ultra-nationalist organization in Turkey with ties to the country’s military and security forces. The name itself represents a mythical place in East-Central Asia where the first Turks are believed to have emerged. Since 2008, over 300 people have been affiliated with the organization in court indictments including those arrested, acquitted and those who have had their homes searched by the police officers. The organization is accused of employing violent measures to destabilize the “unwanted” governments of Turkey.

The Balyoz (Sledgehammer) Trial: “Balyoz” is the name of an alleged military coup that dates back to 2003. According to the allegations, a secularist military coup was planned around 2003, and plans to justify this coup included the bombing of two mosques in Istanbul and accusing Greece of shooting down a military plane over the Aegean Sea. The chaos arising from these two drastic events would then justify the military coup. Furthermore, while the army admitted that the plans had been discussed, but only as a “planning exercise” at a seminar, which bore no possibility of having been carried out in real life. Over 150 people, mostly military members including retired generals and admirals, have been issued arrest warrants as of February 2011.

The KCK (Union of Communities in Kurdistan) Operations: Since April 2009, nearly 2,000 people have been charged with and accused of being members of an illegal organization in the KCK operations carried out by the Turkish security forces. In addition, thousands of others were put in prison without a trial (pre-trial detention). While many academicians, students, human rights lawyers and politicians are among the accused, KCK itself is an organization founded by Abdullah Ocalan to lay the foundations of democratic confederalism.
